A Field Guide to a Nation in Numbers

The country that changed faster than its own statistics.

India is the largest experiment in human development ever attempted. We read it through the data — population, power, prices, particulate matter — drawn from World in Data, MoSPI, RBI, NASA and Copernicus, and tell you what it actually means.

EditionVol. 01 — May 2026 Sources11 open datasets Reading time9 minutes

In 1950 there were 376 million Indians. By 2023 there were 1.43 billion — and India had quietly overtaken China to become the most populous country on Earth.

Numbers this large stop meaning anything. So we anchor them. Every year for seven decades India added, on average, a population the size of Australia. The chart below is not an abstraction; it is the single most consequential demographic event of the modern era, drawn from a century of census records and UN estimates.

What makes the story interesting is not the climb but the bend at the top — the moment the curve begins, almost imperceptibly, to flatten. Fertility has fallen below replacement. The India of 2060 is already visible in the slope of this line.

“You cannot understand a single Indian statistic in isolation. Wealth, air, literacy and power all move together.”

And yet the same period that made India richer also made its air, in places, lethal. Delhi's annual fine-particulate average has hovered near 100 µg/m³ — twenty times the WHO guideline. Growth and harm arrive in the same shipment.

This is the editorial premise of Indica: not to celebrate or condemn, but to place every number beside its companions, cite the source, and let the reader draw the line. The data is open. The interpretation is the work.
