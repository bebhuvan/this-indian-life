# Why does India say GVA when everyone else says GDP?
*The same biscuit packet can carry three different price tags in India’s accounts, depending on who’s counting. Here’s what GVA, GDP, and the three price tags actually mean.*

**Bottom line.** India headlines GVA at basic prices because it shows what producers actually make; GDP at market prices adds the government's product tax bite, showing what buyers pay, and when GST changes, GDP can move even if factories don't produce a single extra unit.

## What on earth are GVA, GDP, basic prices, and factor cost?

In every Indian economy headline, you see two numbers: GDP and GVA. They are not the same, and that small gap explains a lot. Take a biscuit packet. The wheat came from a farmer in Madhya Pradesh, the sugar from a mill in Maharashtra, the packaging from a factory in Gujarat. A baker in Delhi took all that and turned it into biscuits. The baker’s contribution, the value she added, is the selling price of the biscuits minus what she paid for wheat, sugar, and packaging. That is her Gross Value Added, or GVA. If you sum up the value added by every enterprise in the country, from the sabziwala to the software giant, you get India’s total GVA.

But the number you hear most often is GDP, Gross Domestic Product. GDP starts with GVA but then adds the taxes the government levies on products like GST, and subtracts the subsidies it gives on products. So if you buy that biscuit packet at a kirana, the price you pay includes GST; that tax is part of GDP but not part of GVA. The same output, the same biscuits, carry a different price tag depending on what slice of tax or subsidy you include. Before 2015, India’s headline used a third price: factor cost. Factor cost strips away all product taxes and subsidies, leaving only what the factors of production (labour and capital) earned. It was the most bare-bones number, but also the most divorced from the price a buyer actually pays.

## Why does the same biscuit have three different prices in the national accounts?

The biscuit packet is a metaphor, but the concept holds: the same physical biscuit is valued differently depending on which taxes and subsidies you count. Factor cost is what the baker paid for labour, rent, and profit, after deducting any production taxes (like the municipal property tax on the bakery) and adding any production subsidies. That is the income flowing to the factors of production. Basic price is factor cost plus those production taxes, minus production subsidies. It is what the baker receives from the wholesaler, the factory gate price, inclusive of taxes on the production process itself but excluding taxes on the product. Market price is basic price plus product taxes (GST, excise, customs) minus product subsidies (like the subsidy on LPG cylinders). That is the price you pay at the shop.

The Indian national accounts now headline GVA at basic prices and GDP at market prices. So when you read that GVA grew by 7.3% and GDP by 7.4% in 2025-26, the 0.1 percentage point gap is the extra kick from product taxes. India also computes GDP from the expenditure side (what households, firms, and government spent), but the production-side bridge from GVA remains the foundation.

## How do you add up GVA to arrive at GDP?

The numbers as of March 2026 tell the story:

| Component | ₹ lakh crore |
| --- | --- |
| Gross Value Added at basic prices | 323.5 |
| + Net taxes on products (product taxes minus subsidies) | 33.7 |
| = Gross Domestic Product at market prices | 357.1 |

Net taxes on products add a substantial wedge between what producers earn and what buyers spend. When the government raised GST rates on certain items or improved compliance, net product taxes jumped even as real production growth stayed steady. That mechanical effect is why economists watch GVA growth for a purer read on production.

Meanwhile, if you break GVA down by broad sector, you see who is adding value:

| Sector | Share of GVA (2025-26) |
| --- | --- |
| Agriculture, forestry & fishing | 16.8% |
| Industry (including manufacturing, construction, etc.) | 26.8% |
| Services | 56.4% |

Services dominate, but agriculture still provides livelihoods to a huge share of the population despite its small share of value added. That is a reminder that GVA measures output, not employment or well-being.

## Why did India stop saying ‘GDP at factor cost’ in 2015?

Until January 2015, India’s headline was “GDP at factor cost.” As part of the base-year revision from 2004-05 to 2011-12, the National Statistical Office shifted the headline to Gross Value Added at basic prices, with GDP measured at market prices. This brought India in line with the United Nations System of National Accounts 2008. The old factor-cost GDP omitted product taxes and subsidies entirely, so a change in GST or excise duties barely moved the needle on the headline number. Critics argued that made the GDP figure insulated from a big chunk of what the government actually does: tax products. Under the new system, an increase in the GST rate on biscuits shows up immediately as higher GDP, even if the baker produces exactly the same number of biscuits.

## Which number should I care about: GVA or GDP?

It depends on the question you’re asking. If you want the most direct measure of production activity, are more goods being made, more services delivered? Watch real GVA growth. In 2025-26, real GVA rose by 7.3%, a solid pace. If you want to know the total money value of everything produced, including the tax bite the government takes at the point of sale, GDP is your number. GDP at market prices grew 7.4% in real terms that year, slightly more, mostly because net product taxes grew faster than the rest.

The gap can mislead in an almost obvious way: suppose the government slashes GST on a basket of goods. GVA stays the same. The baker still adds the same value, but GDP at market prices shrinks. The headline would scream “slowdown” even though actual production never dipped. Conversely, a GST rate hike boosts GDP without anyone producing more. This is not a theoretical scenario; it played out in the quarterly numbers after the GST implementation in 2017 and during subsequent rate rationalisations. So seasoned policymakers and analysts check both: GVA for the pulse of the real economy, GDP for the full demand picture, from kiranas to export docks.

### Key terms
- **GVA** — Like a bakery’s revenue minus the cost of flour and sugar: the value added by each producer. Not the same as profit, because it includes wages and rent.
- **GDP** — GVA plus taxes on products minus subsidies. The total spending on final goods and services. Not the country’s income, and not the money people receive.
- **basic prices** — The price a producer receives, including taxes on production (like property tax) but before adding GST or excise. Think ‘factory gate price’.
- **factor cost** — The most stripped-down price: what labour and capital earn, with no taxes or subsidies at all. India used this as headline until 2015.
- **market prices** — The price you actually pay, with all product taxes included and subsidies subtracted. GDP is measured at market prices.
- **net product taxes** — Product taxes (like GST) minus product subsidies. The wedge between what buyers pay and what producers receive.

### Achcha, but what does this mean for me?
Okay, let’s bring it to the street. When the TV anchor says ‘GDP grew 7.4%’, part of that jump is just the government collecting more GST. The bakery up the road didn’t necessarily bake an extra biscuit. So if you really want to know whether more stuff got produced (more atta milled, more calls answered, more train tickets sold), look at GVA. That’s your best number for the real, physical economy. GDP tells you the full money value, tax and all, that changed hands in the market. Both matter, but don’t mistake a tax change for a productivity boom.

### What people get wrong
Many assume GDP and GVA are the same, or that GVA excludes all taxes. Actually, GVA at basic prices already includes production taxes like property tax; it’s only product taxes like GST that get added later to make GDP.

### Caveat
GVA and GDP are both nominal unless specified real. Inflation can make either number rise, so always check whether you’re looking at real (inflation-adjusted) or nominal figures. Also, neither GVA nor GDP tells you anything about how the value is distributed across people or regions.
