# India's Heat Deaths Are Not Counted. They Are Estimated. That Is The Problem.

Last updated: 2026-06-03

## Dek

A new study estimates that one nationally extreme heat day could cause about 3,400 excess deaths across India. That is not a body count. But the official counts are not the full truth either.

## Article

A number is doing the rounds: one day of extreme heat could kill about 3,400 people across India.

It is a shocking number. It is also easy to get wrong.

The study behind it, published in *Frontiers in Environmental Health* in 2026, did not count 3,400 bodies on a particular day. It estimated excess deaths: how many more people might die on a day of extreme heat than would normally be expected. For a five-day heatwave, the study estimated nearly 30,000 excess deaths.

That distinction matters. "3,400 people died today from heat" is not what the paper proves. A more careful sentence is: using district mortality rates, 2024 population projections, and heatwave mortality estimates from Indian cities, the study estimates that one nationally extreme heat day could produce about 3,400 excess deaths.

That sentence is less viral. It is also more honest.

But the correction should not be used to dismiss the risk. India's official heat-death numbers are not a full measure of heat mortality either. They usually count deaths that were recognised as heatstroke, sunstroke, heatwave deaths, or extreme-temperature disaster deaths. Heat often kills without leaving that label behind.

It stresses the heart. It worsens respiratory disease. It can trigger kidney injury. It dehydrates people. It raises risk for older adults, infants, pregnant women, outdoor workers, people with chronic illness, and people living in hot, crowded homes without reliable cooling. A person may die during a heatwave and still have the death recorded as a heart attack, stroke, respiratory failure, kidney failure, or with no medically certified cause at all.

So the question is not whether the new model or the official count is "the truth." The question is why a country as heat-exposed as India still cannot answer a basic public-health question directly: how many people does extreme heat kill?

## The Official Numbers Are Lower. They Are Also Narrower.

India has several official or administrative heat-death numbers. They do not agree because they are not counting the same thing.

| Source | Period | Number | What it counts | What it does not count |
| --- | --- | ---: | --- | --- |
| Frontiers model | Single extreme-heat day scenario | about 3,400 excess deaths | Additional deaths above the expected baseline under a modelled extreme-heat scenario | Registered deaths or medically confirmed heatstroke deaths |
| Frontiers model | Five-day heatwave scenario | nearly 30,000 excess deaths | Additional deaths above the expected baseline over a sustained heatwave scenario | A directly observed national death count |
| OWID/EM-DAT | 2024 | 733 reported deaths | Extreme-temperature disaster deaths reported for international disaster accounting | All heat-attributable mortality |
| OWID/EM-DAT | 2000-2024, non-missing India years | 10,398 reported deaths | Reported extreme-temperature disaster deaths across recent non-missing years | The hidden health burden of heat |
| NCRB ADSI | 2023 | about 804 reported deaths | Heat/sunstroke deaths recorded in an accidental-death framework | Excess deaths during heatwaves |
| IMD DWE | 2024 | 460 reported deaths | Heatwave deaths in IMD's meteorological disaster-event compilation | Medical death registration or all-cause excess mortality |
| NCDC/NHRIDS | 2024, March-July public reporting | about 161 confirmed deaths | Confirmed heatstroke deaths entering the surveillance system by that public-reporting cutoff | Heat-attributable deaths outside diagnosis, facilities, or reporting |
| NCDC/NHRIDS parliamentary reporting | 2024-03-01 to 2024-07-27 | 374 reported deaths | Heatstroke deaths cited in cutoff-specific parliamentary/public reporting | Full-year heat-attributable mortality |

This table should not be read from top to bottom as if every row were measuring the same thing. The Frontiers rows estimate excess deaths. NCRB and IMD report labelled administrative or disaster deaths. NCDC reports heatstroke surveillance deaths. OWID/EM-DAT records disaster deaths for international comparison. The disagreement is not a rounding problem. It is a definition problem.

Our World in Data's EM-DAT-based disaster dataset reports India's "Extreme temperature" deaths at a much lower scale than the new model. For 2000-2024, the India total across years with non-missing values is 10,398. Recent values include 1,930 in 2002, 1,351 in 2003, 2,248 in 2015, 300 in 2016, and 733 in 2024.

NCRB's *Accidental Deaths and Suicides in India* reports heat/sunstroke deaths under accidental deaths due to forces of nature. Public reporting on the 2023 NCRB report put heat/sunstroke deaths at about 804.

IMD's *Disastrous Weather Events 2024* report gives another official number: 460 reported heatwave deaths in 2024. We parsed the PDF and checked it three ways. Table 2 reports Heat Wave casualties of 460. Table 7's monthly heatwave deaths sum to 460: 1 in March, 39 in April, 185 in May, and 235 in June. Table 22's state table also sums to 460, led by Uttar Pradesh with 240, Bihar with 63, Telangana with 46, and Jharkhand with 35.

That IMD number is useful, but it has a clear boundary. The report itself says the information is sourced from media reports and government Disaster Management Authority reports, with data-collection limitations. It is an official meteorological disaster-event count. It is not a medical death registry and not an excess-mortality estimate.

NCDC's heat illness surveillance gives another layer. Public reporting from the National Heat-Related Illness and Death Surveillance system cited roughly 48,000 suspected heatstroke cases in 2024 and about 161 confirmed heatstroke deaths. A parliamentary/wire report cited 374 heatstroke deaths and 67,637 suspected cases up to July 27, 2024. These figures can differ because they use different cutoffs, definitions, confirmation rules, and reporting channels.

This is the first lesson: India does not have one heat-death number. It has several numbers, each answering a different question. All are useful. None is the same as national heat-attributable mortality.

## Excess Deaths Ask A Different Question

Heat is a classic excess-mortality problem.

During a severe heatwave, researchers do not only ask how many death certificates mention heatstroke. They ask how many more people died than would have died under normal conditions.

This is the logic behind excess mortality. It does not identify exactly which person died because of heat. Instead, it estimates whether deaths rose above the expected baseline during heat exposure.

That makes it powerful. It also makes it uncertain.

An excess-mortality estimate depends on the baseline death rate, the heat definition, the exposure window, the population exposed, and the estimated relationship between heat and mortality. Change those assumptions and the answer can change.

The Frontiers paper used district-level mortality rates from the Civil Registration System, 2024 population projections, and heatwave mortality coefficients adapted from a 10-city Indian study. It then transferred those city-based risk estimates to districts using climate-zone similarity.

That is a serious first-pass national estimate. It is not a final answer.

## A Scale Check: How Thousands Become Mathematically Possible

The point of a simple sensitivity check is not to validate the Frontiers paper. It is to make the scale legible.

India's 2024 population was about 1.45 billion. Using a rounded World Bank crude death rate of 6.6 deaths per 1,000 people per year gives roughly 26,000 baseline deaths per day nationally. On a normal day, in a country this large, tens of thousands of people die from all causes.

If heat temporarily raises mortality among an exposed share of the population, the arithmetic can move quickly.

| Scenario | Exposed share of population | Mortality lift among exposed baseline deaths | Illustrative excess deaths in one day | If sustained for five days |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Very low | 20% | 3.0% | about 157 | about 787 |
| Low | 35% | 5.0% | about 459 | about 2,296 |
| Middle | 50% | 10.0% | about 1,312 | about 6,559 |
| Frontiers-scale denominator check | 70% | 18.5% | about 3,400 | about 17,000 |
| High | 75% | 15.0% | about 2,952 | about 14,758 |

This table is deliberately simple. It does not model districts, age, humidity, night-time heat, exposure duration, lagged deaths, harvesting, rural-urban differences, or medical vulnerability. It should not be described as an independent estimate of India's heat deaths.

The five-day column is only the one-day sensitivity number multiplied by five. It is not the same as the Frontiers paper's five-day heatwave scenario, which is a separate modelled estimate of nearly 30,000 excess deaths.

But it shows why the 3,400 number is not absurd on scale alone. If a very large population is exposed, a temporary mortality increase of a few percent produces hundreds of excess deaths. A larger exposure footprint and a larger mortality increase can produce thousands.

It also shows why the exact number remains uncertain. The answer depends on who is exposed, how hot it is, how long the heat lasts, whether nights cool down, how humid the air is, what people are doing, and whether they can protect themselves.

## Why The Estimate Is Plausible

The first reason is scale: India has 1.45 billion people. Even a small increase in daily mortality can become a large absolute number when applied across a very large exposed population.

The second reason is exposure. IMD reported 554 cumulative heatwave days over the Indian region in 2024, compared with 230 in 2023. UNDP Human Climate Horizons estimates India's median days over 95F at about 79.8 days per year in the 1986-2005 baseline, rising to about 181.2 days per year by 2080-2099 under RCP8.5.

CEEW's 2025 district heat-risk study strengthens the same point from another angle. It maps heat risk across 734 districts using 35 indicators covering hazard, exposure, and vulnerability. It goes beyond daytime temperature by including night-time heat and relative humidity, and combines 12 km IMDAA climate data with satellite imagery, NFHS 2019-21, and Census 2011. Its headline finding is that about 57% of Indian districts, home to 76% of the population, are at high to very high heat risk. This is not mortality evidence. It is evidence that the risk environment is widespread and uneven.

The third reason is work. World Bank data put agriculture at about 41.6% of India's employment in 2025, and vulnerable employment at about 71.6%. The Lancet Countdown's India 2025 data sheet estimates that heat exposure in 2024 caused 247 billion potential labour hours lost, or 419 hours per person. That was 124% higher than in 1990-1999. Agriculture accounted for 66% of those losses and construction for 20%.

These are not death counts. But they describe the risk environment. Many Indians are exposed to outdoor or poorly protected heat, and many cannot simply stop working when the temperature becomes dangerous.

The fourth reason is existing health stress. World Bank data put India's mean PM2.5 exposure at about 48.4 micrograms per cubic metre in 2020. Its air-pollution mortality indicator was 139.3 deaths per 100,000 population in 2019. Heat and pollution are different hazards, but both stress the cardiovascular and respiratory systems. A hot day in a polluted city is not just a warmer version of a clean day.

Cooling is also more complicated than a single access statistic. Electricity access is high in national datasets, but electricity access is not the same as reliable cooling. A fan is not an air conditioner. An air conditioner is not useful during a power cut. Ownership is not the same as affordability. A household can be electrified and still be dangerous during a hot night.

So the 3,400 estimate is not implausible on its face. A country with India's population, heat exposure, outdoor work, baseline disease burden, air pollution, and uneven cooling protection can plausibly see thousands of excess deaths during severe heat.

## Why The Estimate Is Still Uncertain

The paper's weakness is not that it models. All national heat mortality estimates require modelling. The weakness is what had to be assumed because better mortality data are not publicly available.

First, the mortality-risk coefficients come from Indian cities. Applying them to all districts is useful, but rural heat risk may not behave like urban heat risk. Rural workers may spend more time outdoors. Cities may have stronger urban heat islands. Cities may also have better hospitals and higher AC penetration. The direction of bias is not obvious.

Second, district-average heat exposure hides local conditions. A district can contain shaded areas, dense settlements, tin-roofed homes, worksites, informal housing, and urban heat islands. Heat exposure is intensely local.

Third, dry-bulb temperature is not enough. Humidity changes how heat affects the body. A dry 43C day and a humid 38C day can be dangerous in different ways. Heat index, Wet Bulb Globe Temperature, or similar metrics may better capture health and labour risk.

Fourth, nights matter. A dangerous heatwave is not only about the afternoon maximum. If nights stay hot, bodies do not recover, homes remain hot, sleep is disrupted, and risk accumulates.

Fifth, India does not publish the daily mortality data needed for direct validation. A strong test would use daily all-cause deaths by district, age, sex, and place of death, linked to daily heat exposure. That is not available as a public national dataset.

Sixth, cause-of-death certification is incomplete. Public reporting and recent research cite India's Medically Certified Cause of Death coverage at 22.5% of registered deaths in 2020. If most deaths do not have medically certified causes, official heatstroke counts cannot settle the debate.

These caveats do not make the 3,400 estimate useless. They tell us how to read it: as a scenario estimate under imperfect but visible assumptions.

## The Measurement Gap Is The Story

The public debate can easily become careless in two directions.

One side may repeat the 3,400 number as if it were a direct daily death count. That is wrong.

The other side may point to official heatstroke or IMD disaster deaths and say the model is alarmist. That is also wrong.

The honest position is more uncomfortable: India likely has a large hidden burden of heat-related mortality, and the current data systems are not designed to measure it well.

CRS tells us about registered deaths, but annual death registration is not a daily heat-mortality system. SRS gives valuable mortality rates, but not event-level deaths. MCCD gives medically certified causes, but coverage is low. NCRB gives administrative cause labels, not excess deaths. NCDC surveillance is essential, but it is facility-based and not yet a fully public national daily dataset. IMD reports disaster-event deaths, not all-cause mortality. EM-DAT is a disaster database, not a public-health attribution system.

Each source captures a part of the problem. None captures the whole burden.

## What Better Evidence Would Look Like

A serious heat mortality system would not depend on one dramatic paper every few years.

It would publish daily all-cause deaths by district, age, sex, and place of death, with privacy protection. It would publish clear metadata on missingness and reporting delays. It would link deaths to IMD heat metrics, including maximum temperature, minimum temperature, humidity, and heat index or WBGT. It would expand medical certification of cause of death. It would strengthen NCDC heat illness surveillance and make aggregate time series public. It would audit deaths during severe heatwave windows, not only deaths where someone wrote "heatstroke."

It would also track vulnerability: outdoor work, informal employment, housing type, fan and AC access, power reliability, water access, age, disability, and chronic disease burden. CEEW's district heat-risk work is useful here because it shows how such vulnerability mapping can be made district-specific rather than left as a generic national warning.

That is the data architecture needed to answer the question directly.

Until then, estimates will remain necessary.

## What The Data Can And Cannot Say

The data can say that the Frontiers number is a modelled excess-death estimate, not a count of bodies. They can say that IMD reported 460 heatwave disaster-event deaths in its 2024 DWE report, and that the figure reconciles across its annual, monthly, and state tables. They can say that OWID/EM-DAT reported extreme-temperature deaths are much lower than modelled excess-mortality scenarios. They can say that India has large heat exposure, large outdoor-work exposure, high vulnerable employment, serious air-pollution stress, and uneven cooling protection. They can say that district-level heat risk is widespread: CEEW estimates that 57% of districts, home to 76% of India's population, are at high to very high heat risk.

The data cannot say that 3,400 Indians die from heat every day. They cannot say that the Frontiers paper counted actual bodies. They cannot say that official heatstroke counts are fake. They cannot say that IMD's 460 reported deaths measure all heat-attributable mortality. They cannot say that the true burden is exactly 3,400 deaths on any given day.

## The Honest Conclusion

The 3,400-deaths-in-a-day estimate should be treated carefully. It is not a direct count. It is not proof that exactly 3,400 people died on a specific day. It depends on transferred risk coefficients and imperfect district data.

But official heatstroke and heatwave counts should be treated carefully too. They are not proof that only a few hundred people died from heat. They are narrow administrative counts in a country where many deaths are not medically certified and where heat often kills through other medical causes.

The best reading is this: India's true heat mortality burden is probably far larger than official heatstroke deaths suggest. The exact size remains uncertain because the data needed to measure it directly are incomplete, fragmented, and not public enough. The new study is not the final answer. It is a warning about what our current systems cannot see.

That should make the number less sensational, not less serious.
