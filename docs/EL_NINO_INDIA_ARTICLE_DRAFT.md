# When El Nino Comes For The Monsoon

Last updated: 2026-06-06

Working URL slug: `what-el-nino-does-to-india`

## Dek

India enters the 2026 monsoon with a below-normal rainfall forecast and rising El Nino risk. History says that combination is dangerous. It also says it is not destiny.

## Short Answer

El Nino raises the risk of a weak Indian summer monsoon, but it does not mechanically produce drought. In the 1950-2025 record we joined from NOAA and IMD, 12 of 26 El Nino monsoons finished at least 5% below normal and 8 finished at least 10% below normal. But 11 El Nino monsoons still ended above normal. The Pacific matters. So do the Indian Ocean Dipole, the timing of rain, regional distribution, irrigation, reservoirs, stocks, trade policy, and prices.

The honest version is: El Nino is a warning light, not a verdict.

## The Article

It is June 2026, and India is again watching the monsoon with unusual attention.

The India Meteorological Department's long-range forecast put the 2026 southwest monsoon at about 92% of the long-period average, with a model error of plus or minus 5%. That is a below-normal forecast. At the same time, official ENSO forecasts have been pointing toward El Nino conditions emerging during the northern-hemisphere summer. IMD's April forecast also noted that neutral Indian Ocean Dipole conditions were present, with a positive IOD likely toward the end of the monsoon season.

That combination is why 2026 should not be treated as a footnote. It is the reason to ask the bigger question.

When the Pacific warms in an El Nino pattern, what actually happens to India?

The simple answer is familiar: El Nino often weakens the Indian monsoon. The better answer is more useful: El Nino raises the odds of a bad monsoon, but the final result depends on when the warming arrives, how strong it becomes, whether the Indian Ocean helps or hurts, and where the rain falls inside India.

That distinction matters because the monsoon is not an abstract weather event. It is a working system. It waters fields. It refills reservoirs. It decides sowing conditions. It changes rural incomes. It moves prices of pulses, cereals, vegetables, edible oils, milk, and sugar. It forces government decisions on exports, imports, procurement, stocks, and inflation control.

The question for 2026, therefore, is not only whether El Nino forms.

The real question is whether India's rain, crops, reservoirs, and food markets can absorb the shock if it does.

### What El Nino Is, Without The Jargon

El Nino is a warming of the central and eastern equatorial Pacific Ocean. That sounds far away from India because it is. But the tropical atmosphere is connected. When the Pacific warms, rainfall and pressure patterns shift across the tropics. The Indian monsoon can lose some of the large-scale support it normally gets.

The key phrase is "can lose." Not "will lose."

India's monsoon is powered by the seasonal heating difference between land and ocean, moisture from the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal, low-pressure systems, the monsoon trough, and regional circulation. El Nino pushes against that system. It does not control every part of it.

That is why a useful article cannot stop at "El Nino equals drought." It has to ask what the data says year by year.

### The Historical Test: What Happened To Rain In El Nino Years?

We joined NOAA's ENSO record to IMD's all-India southwest monsoon rainfall record for 1950-2025. For each year, we classified the monsoon season using the active ENSO phase during the monsoon months and compared it with IMD's June-September rainfall departure.

The pattern is clear, but not absolute.

Across 1950-2025, El Nino monsoons averaged a rainfall departure of -3.2%. La Nina monsoons averaged +5.9%. Neutral monsoons averaged +2.0%.

That is the first big result: El Nino years are visibly drier on average.

But the distribution matters more than the average. Of the 26 El Nino monsoons in the joined record, 15 finished below normal, 12 were at least 5% below normal, and 8 were at least 10% below normal. At the same time, 11 El Nino monsoons finished above normal.

That is the second big result: El Nino raises risk, but it does not decide the monsoon alone.

### Why 1997 Is The Year That Stops Bad Storytelling

The 1997 El Nino was strong. If El Nino mechanically caused Indian drought, 1997 should have been a simple drought story.

It was not. India's all-India monsoon rainfall departure in 1997 was +0.2%, essentially near normal in the IMD all-India record. The Indian Ocean Dipole was positive during the monsoon, with the JJAS DMI averaging 0.48 C and the Sep-Nov window reaching 0.97 C in our NOAA DMI artifact.

A positive IOD can change the regional ocean-atmosphere pattern in a way that supports Indian rainfall. It does not guarantee a good monsoon, but it is one reason El Nino years can surprise people.

That is why 2026 needs IOD in the opening dashboard. If readers only watch the Pacific, they are watching only half the climate signal.

### The National Average Hides The Farmer's Problem

India does not eat an all-India average. It eats rice from eastern and southern regions, wheat from the northwest and central belt, pulses from rainfed regions, oilseeds from specific agro-climatic zones, and vegetables from supply chains that can break after too much rain or too little rain.

That is why all-India rainfall is only the first chart.

The IMD homogeneous-region data lets us split the monsoon into Northwest India, Central India, South Peninsula, and East/Northeast India. The subdivision data goes further. For the first article, the homogeneous-region chart is probably enough. It keeps the page understandable. The subdivision data should power a future interactive map or state-focused follow-up.

### Rainfall Becomes Food Through Crops

The monsoon matters because agriculture still matters.

It is not because every acre is unirrigated or because every crop depends on June rain in the same way. The point is more basic: a large part of India's cropping calendar, rural labour demand, reservoir recharge, groundwater recharge, and food-price risk still runs through the monsoon.

The crop data gives us the next link in the chain. We joined IMD all-India monsoon rainfall with DES crop area, production, and yield tables from 1950-51 through 2024-25, and with UPAg dashboard data through 2025-26 advance estimates.

For foodgrains, the correlation between monsoon rainfall departure and production growth is 0.585 in the DES historical panel. For rice, it is 0.577. For nine oilseeds, it is 0.517. Pulses are lower at 0.387, but still positive. Wheat is lower because it is a rabi crop, more irrigated, and responds to a different weather calendar.

This does not mean rainfall alone explains production. It means the monsoon remains visible in the crop record even after decades of irrigation, technology, procurement, and market change.

### Rainfall Becomes Prices Through Scarcity, Stocks, And Policy

Food inflation is where monsoon risk becomes visible to urban households.

But prices are not just rainfall with a lag. They also respond to stocks, imports, exports, procurement, transport, local floods, heat, disease, and policy. Vegetables can spike in a good-rainfall year because too much rain damages supply chains. Pulses can spike after production stress. Cereals can be held down by public stocks, procurement, or trade controls.

That is why the price section should be honest. The monsoon is one pressure. It is not the only pressure.

In the MoSPI food-inflation panel we built, 2015 and 2023 show the kind of story readers understand immediately.

In 2015, an El Nino year with a -12.7% all-India monsoon departure, average post-monsoon onion inflation in October-December was 46.51%, tomato inflation was 27.77%, while rice inflation was slightly negative. That is a supply-chain and commodity-specific story, not a generic "food went up" story.

In 2023, an El Nino year with a -5.3% all-India monsoon departure and a positive IOD, post-monsoon consumer food price inflation averaged 8.28%. Rice inflation averaged 11.93%, vegetables 16.05%, and onion 67.56%.

Then 2024 warns against overfitting rainfall. The all-India monsoon was surplus in our panel, but post-monsoon vegetable inflation was very high. That tells the reader something important: rainfall quantity is not the whole food-inflation story. Distribution, crop-specific shocks, storage, and policy can dominate.

### The Policy Layer: India Does Not Just Watch The Monsoon

India responds to food stress. That response is part of the monsoon story.

When cereal, pulse, onion, sugar, or edible-oil risks rise, policy can move quickly: export restrictions, import duty changes, stock limits, open-market sales, procurement changes, buffer releases, and anti-hoarding measures. These actions do not erase a bad monsoon. But they can change who feels the shock, when prices rise, and how sharply the spike reaches consumers.

This section needs a verified timeline before publication. It should not rely on memory or generic policy claims. For each major case year, we should cite official sources from PIB, DGFT, Ministry of Consumer Affairs, Ministry of Agriculture, RBI, and IMD.

### Reservoirs And Groundwater Are The Missing Middle

Rainfall deficit matters differently depending on storage.

A weak monsoon after full reservoirs is not the same as a weak monsoon after a dry year. Irrigated wheat is not the same as rainfed pulses. A national rainfall number does not tell us whether tanks, reservoirs, canals, and groundwater are ready for the season.

For publication, we should either ingest CWC reservoir bulletins or include a clear data-gap box. The best source is CWC's reservoir storage bulletin page and RSMS-linked bulletin PDFs. If we digitize these, the article can show reservoir storage entering the 2026 monsoon compared with historical averages.

### What To Watch In 2026

The 2026 story should be updated through the season, but the indicators are already clear.

Watch the monsoon's actual progress, not just the seasonal forecast. Did Kerala onset happen on time? Did the monsoon advance stall? Did June rainfall miss the northwest, central India, or the peninsula? Did July recover the deficit? Did August go dry? Was September a rescue month?

Watch ENSO and IOD together. If El Nino strengthens while IOD remains neutral or negative, the risk profile is different from an El Nino year with a strong positive IOD.

Watch region and timing. A national rainfall deficit is bad, but a long dry spell during sowing in a major kharif belt can be worse than the final all-India number suggests.

Watch reservoirs. Rainfall deficits become more damaging when storage is weak.

Watch crop sowing and advance estimates. UPAg's progressive area-sown data can help track whether farmers are delaying, switching, or reducing area.

Watch food prices by component. Cereals, pulses, vegetables, oils, and milk do not move together. The article should keep them separate.

### The Core Argument

The best one-sentence thesis is:

> El Nino makes a weak Indian monsoon more likely, but India's actual damage depends on the Indian Ocean, regional rainfall, crop exposure, water storage, and policy response.

That is the line the whole article should defend.

It respects the data. It is simple enough for a lay reader. It is also strong enough for a researcher to take seriously because it avoids the lazy version of the story.

## Methodology

This article uses source-first local data artifacts. The core climate, crop, and price claims should be reproducible from files in `data/series/` and scripts in `scripts/`.

### ENSO

ENSO history comes from NOAA CPC's Oceanic Nino Index feed, ingested by `scripts/ingest-noaa-enso.mjs` into `data/series/noaa-enso.global.oni_seasonal.json`. The current artifact contains 916 seasonal rows from 1950-DJF through 2026-MAM.

For monsoon-year analysis, the derived join uses MJJ, JJA, JAS, and ASO ONI seasons as the monsoon-relevant window. The article uses the active ENSO phase during the monsoon from `data/series/derived.IN.climate.enso_iod_imd_monsoon_join.json`.

### Indian Ocean Dipole

IOD history comes from NOAA PSL's Dipole Mode Index file, ingested by `scripts/ingest-noaa-iod-dmi.mjs` into `data/series/noaa-iod.global.dmi_monthly.json`. The current artifact contains monthly DMI rows from 1870-01 to 2025-04.

The derived monsoon join calculates DMI averages over June-September, July-September, August-October, and September-November windows. For article explanation, it marks DMI >= 0.4 C as positive IOD, DMI <= -0.4 C as negative IOD, and values between them as neutral.

### Rainfall

Historical monsoon rainfall comes from IMD Pune's public southwest monsoon rainfall tables, ingested by `scripts/ingest-imd-monsoon-rainfall.mjs` into `data/series/imd.IN.climate.imd.monsoon_rainfall_regions.json`. The artifact covers all-India and four homogeneous regions from 1901 through 2025.

Meteorological-subdivision rainfall comes from IMD Pune's public subdivision rainfall table, ingested by `scripts/ingest-imd-subdivision-rainfall.mjs` into `data/series/imd.IN.climate.imd.subdivision_rainfall.json`. This is useful for maps and local follow-ups, but the first article should use homogeneous regions for readability.

For 2026, the article should use IMD forecasts, press releases, and monthly climate summaries. Do not mix incomplete 2026 live rainfall with completed historical annual monsoon outcomes without clear labelling.

### Crops

Long crop area, production, and yield data come from DES Agricultural Statistics at a Glance 2024-25, ingested by `scripts/ingest-des-agri-apy-glance.mjs` into `data/series/des-agri.IN.agriculture.all_india_crop_apy.json`. The DES artifact covers major crops from 1950-51 through 2024-25.

Recent crop and broader dashboard data come from UPAg public Dash endpoints, ingested by `scripts/ingest-upag-dash-apy.py`. The all-India UPAg artifact covers 37 crops from 1966-67 through 2025-26 and labels 2025-26 as Third Advance Estimates.

The rainfall-crop panel is derived by `scripts/derive-rainfall-crop-panel.mjs` into `data/series/derived.IN.agriculture.rainfall_crop_apy_panel.json`. It joins all-India monsoon rainfall and ENSO fields to crop-year rows and adds year-over-year and previous-five-year deviation metrics.

### Prices

Food inflation data comes from MoSPI/eSankhyiki CPI artifacts already in `data/series/`. The monsoon-food inflation panel is created by `scripts/derive-monsoon-food-inflation-panel.mjs` into `data/series/derived.IN.prices.monsoon_food_inflation_panel.json`.

The panel covers 2012-2025 and computes four windows: June-September monsoon, October-December post-monsoon, January-March rabi price window, and the following July-June food year. It includes headline CPI, Consumer Food Price Index, food and beverages, cereals, pulses, vegetables, oils, milk, spices, rice, wheat atta, pulses item, onion, tomato, potato, and mustard oil where available.

For longer inflation context, use `data/series/worldbank.IN.prices.cpi.headline_annual_long.json`, which uses World Bank annual headline CPI inflation from 1960 onward and splices later full-year MoSPI values when World Bank has not yet published the year. Do not use this long headline series as if it were food-component inflation.

### Correlations

Reported crop correlations are simple Pearson correlations between IMD all-India monsoon rainfall departure and crop output metrics in the DES historical panel. They are descriptive. They are not causal estimates and do not control for irrigation, technology, crop area, prices, fertilizer, procurement, heat, pests, or policy.

The article should treat 2026 as a live context, not as a completed observation. The opening can cite the IMD seasonal forecast and ENSO outlook. The historical charts should run through completed data, currently 2025 for IMD monsoon rainfall and 2026-MAM for observed ONI. ## Caveats, Blind Spots, And Counterarguments

### El Nino Is Not A Single Indian Forecast

The biggest possible misuse of this article is to say: "El Nino means India will have drought." The historical record does not support that. It supports a weaker statement: El Nino raises drought risk.

### All-India Rainfall Can Hide Damage

All-India June-September rainfall is useful because it is long, official, and comparable. But it can hide a bad sowing window, a regional dry spell, an extreme rain event, or a crop-specific shock. A future version should add gridded IMD rainfall maps.

### IOD Is A Modifier, Not A Magic Shield

1997 is a strong warning against deterministic El Nino storytelling, but it should not be turned into a new deterministic story where positive IOD automatically saves the monsoon. 1972 had a positive IOD signal and still produced a severe all-India rainfall deficit. The ocean-atmosphere system is not one lever.

### Crop Output Is Not Only Rainfall

Irrigation, crop area, seed varieties, fertilizer, pest outbreaks, procurement, MSP expectations, and prices all affect production. The rainfall-crop panel shows association, not proof that rainfall caused every production movement.

### Food Inflation Is Not Only Crop Output

Vegetables can spike after excess rain. Edible oils can move with global prices. Cereals can be influenced by stocks and policy. Pulses can respond to imports, domestic output, and trade restrictions. Food inflation is a chain, not a single climate variable.

### 2026 Could Change Quickly

June 2026 is too early to know the final monsoon. The article should be written so it can be updated: forecast, onset, June rainfall, July rainfall, August rainfall, September rainfall, reservoirs, sowing, advance estimates, and food prices.

### Data Vintage Matters

IMD historical monsoon rainfall is complete through 2025 in the current artifact. NOAA ONI observations are ingested through 2026-MAM. MoSPI 2012-base food inflation artifacts run through 2025 in the derived panel; separate 2024-base CPI sources may be needed for 2026 food-price updates.
